# English Prose

Use this reference for English drafting, revision, polishing, and review.

## Core Rule

English prose should be direct, specific, source-grounded, and durable. A
style pass must improve readability without changing evidence, terminology,
scope, or claim strength.

Good English prose makes the actor, action, object, decision, evidence, and
consequence visible where they matter. It does not hide weak support behind a
more confident voice.

## Claim Strength

Treat unsupported significance as a prose defect, not as wording to polish.
Remove, narrow, ground, or flag claims such as:

- pivotal, transformative, groundbreaking, crucial, enduring;
- broad "platform shift" or "industry trend" framing;
- generic claims that work improves clarity, consistency, collaboration, or
  value without evidence;
- future-outlook endings that promise scale, impact, or stakeholder value
  without a source.

Prefer concrete facts:

- what changed;
- who decided it;
- what file, workflow, command, or artifact now exists;
- what evidence supports the status;
- what next action is known.

If the source does not establish a claim, do not make the sentence merely more
executive-ready. Say the claim is unsupported, or rewrite to the supported
fact.

Pressure to sound confident, polished, strategic, human, or stakeholder-ready
does not lower the evidence bar. Do not replace a claim like "pivotal",
"groundbreaking", or "transformative" with softer unsupported claims such as
"strengthens the workflow", "gives teams a practical path", "creates durable
communication", or "improves collaboration" unless the source establishes that
effect. If only the artifact's existence is supported, say only that the
artifact exists or report the unsupported claim.

## Source Grounding

Preserve the source boundary:

- Do not invent dates, owners, citations, metrics, blockers, approvals, or
  causal explanations.
- Replace vague attribution such as unnamed observers, experts, critics,
  reports, or sources with a named source when one is available.
- If evidence is absent, either remove the claim or mark it as unresolved.
- Keep uncertainty where it is real; do not convert possible, planned, or
  inferred work into completed fact.

## Anti-AI Cleanup

Remove chat-response wrappers and durable-document artifacts:

- "Here is...", "Let's...", "I hope this helps", "Would you like...";
- process narration about the rewrite itself;
- knowledge-cutoff disclaimers;
- apology or reassurance language;
- diff narration outside changelogs, release notes, migration notes, or review
  summaries.

Watch for AI-pattern clusters:

- rule-of-three padding;
- synonym cycling for the same concept;
- fake contrast such as "not just X, but Y";
- formulaic "from X to Y" ranges;
- abstract nouns stacked around "clarity", "alignment", "impact",
  "landscape", or "value";
- decorative headings, excessive bold, title-case drift, emoji, or dramatic
  sentence fragments.

These are heuristics. Do not flag one polished sentence, one transition word,
or one passive construction unless it harms accuracy, readability, or source
grounding.

## Sentence Quality

Prefer:

- simple verbs over inflated substitutes;
- consistent terminology over ornamental variation;
- active voice when the actor matters;
- passive voice when the actor is unknown, irrelevant, or intentionally
  omitted;
- sentence-case headings unless the document family requires otherwise;
- straight punctuation unless a quote, title, or source text requires
  otherwise.

Avoid filler such as "in order to", "due to the fact that", "at this point in
time", and "it is important to note". Keep hedges only when the uncertainty is
real.

## Executive And Stakeholder Prose

Executive-ready does not mean more certain than the evidence allows. For
status, update, and stakeholder prose:

- keep the body concise and scannable;
- separate confirmed facts from inference;
- name material risks plainly;
- avoid making normal review activity sound like a blocker;
- avoid turning a status update into a changelog dump;
- preserve owner-owned health, action mode, issue evidence, and mutation
  semantics when the draft comes from another workflow.

## False Positives

Do not overcorrect:

- quoted text or source-preserved wording;
- formal vocabulary that belongs to the domain;
- concise prose that is already clear;
- intentional voice in publication prose;
- correct formatting from a template;
- one isolated style signal that does not reduce clarity or accuracy.

When in doubt, prefer a narrow finding over a broad rewrite.
